2 · The ceremony — pin whom you watch (once)

Enter the community id and the log's public key. Both must reach you out of band — two secretaries at a federation meeting, a phone call, paper — and never from the request you are asked to sign: a request that could name its own judge would always pass. This is the ceremony; everything after it is arithmetic.

3 · Co-sign a checkpoint

The committee sends you witness-request.json whenever their log grows. Drop it here. The page checks four things: the head is from the community you watch; it is signed by the log key from the ceremony; it roots the log that came with it; and that history extends the one your memory pins. All four hold — you get cosig-N.json to send back, and an updated witness.json to keep. Any of them fails — you get the reason, and nothing is signed.

What this page cannot protect you from — said plainly

Losing the file: witness.json is the key and the memory. Lose it and this witness is gone — tell the committee, make a new one, redo the ceremony. And whoever holds a copy can sign in your name: treat it like the society's chequebook. A stale memory: after co-signing, keep the new file. Sign with an old one and you will still refuse rewrites — but your memory pins less history than you actually vouched for. This machine: a compromised browser could sign something other than what it shows you. The request and the co-signature are small JSON files — anyone can re-check them with proto/clubvote.py witness, which is the same witness, and two witnesses on two machines is the whole point.
