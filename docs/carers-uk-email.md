# Covering email to Carers UK — a draft, kept in the open

*The covering email for [the adviser's note](https://glassontin.github.io/civic-kernel/adviser-note.html),
drafted like every letter in this project: public before it is sent, with the personal
details travelling only with the sent copy. The note is the letter; this earns the click.*

---

**Subject:** A benefits check your advisers can inspect, not trust — built around the
earnings limit

Dear [name — or "the Carers UK advice team"],

I'm writing with one page and one link, both free and with no product behind them.

The link is **Owed** — a benefits check for pension-age support and Carer's Allowance
that runs entirely on the client's own device. It makes no network request at all:
nothing a carer types goes anywhere, there's no account, and it works offline. Every
question names the rule that lets it ask, every amount shows its working with links to
legislation.gov.uk, and the client can save their answers as a small file they keep —
nothing exists unless they choose to save it.

The reason I'm writing to Carers UK first is the earnings limit. Behind the tool sits
a public study of fifty-two institutional failures, one of them the overpayment
scandal you've campaigned on — the state holding the earnings data while the debt
grew, then prosecuting the growth. Owed is meant to be the other half of that finding:
the limit checked privately, before the first pound of overpayment, by the person it
protects. It competes with nothing you run — done well, its job is to bring a carer to
your helpline sooner, with a saved file in hand instead of a fear.

The attached one-page note for advisers covers what it computes, why it can be checked
rather than trusted, and where it honestly stops — eight entitlements, not advice, no
Universal Credit or PIP, and no replacement for the full calculators your team already
uses.

The ask is small: fifteen minutes from someone on your advice team, running your
hardest anonymised cases through it next to the Turn2us check your site links. Where
it disagrees, or asks a question badly, I'd genuinely like to know — the rules are
public data, and fixable. If it's useful, it's yours to share.

With thanks for everything your advice line carries,

[Ian Williams]
