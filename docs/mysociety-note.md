# A note to mySociety — a draft, kept in the open

*Like everything in this project, the letter is public before it is sent:
[github.com/GlassOnTin/civic-kernel](https://github.com/GlassOnTin/civic-kernel).
Direct contact details travel with the sent copy, not the repo.*

---

Two of your tools sit exactly where a design study I've been running kept arriving,
and I wanted you to have the evidence, because it is partly evidence about your own
work.

The study rebuilt sixty-one documented institutional failures — Horizon, the deleted
WhatsApps, the FOI Clearing House, the carer prosecuted for a debt the state watched
grow — as machine-checked case files, each asking one narrow question: *which record,
required at which moment, citing which rule, would have changed this?* Every file
cites its sources (168 real-world citations across the corpus) and ends in an honest
verdict; eight of the sixty-one are marked **breaks**, because the method counts its
own failures.

**WhatDoTheyKnow is halfway through one of the corpus's strongest findings.** The
FOI case file concludes that every slow-walking lever — the missed deadline, the
stacked public-interest extension, the profiled requester — acts on the *request*,
so the end-state is a record public by default, with nothing left to request. Your
platform already does the first half at scale: it makes the request and the response
public, which is what turned slow-walking from a private frustration into a
measurable pattern. The case file is, in effect, the argument for where that road
ends — and the sixty-one-case corpus is evidence your FOI work could cite.

**FixMyStreet is the citizen's half of a shape the corpus met twice and named.**
Two case files — Grenfell's ignored hazard reports, and SEND assessment requests
rationed by delay — reduce to the same missing structure: an inbound report should
produce a dated receipt, the receipt should start a duty-clock, and a breached clock
should carry a consequence by default. Your platform already produces the public,
dated report and makes the silence visible. What the corpus adds is the name for the
missing half — the duty-clock with a default on breach — and the documented cost of
its absence.

Both findings, with ten others, are staged into a
[roadmap for UK law](https://glassontin.github.io/civic-kernel/roadmap.html)
(publication by default is step five; self-enforcing deadlines step six), each step
grounded in its cases. The corpus itself is at
[scenarios](https://glassontin.github.io/civic-kernel/scenarios.html), the
synthesis at [map](https://glassontin.github.io/civic-kernel/map.html), and the
method — including how cases are chosen and refused — at
[triage](https://glassontin.github.io/civic-kernel/docs/triage.md).

The ask is small: a look, and corrections. If your research team finds the encoding
method useful, or finds it wrong somewhere, either is valuable — the case files are
public data and the door for proposing or disputing a case is
[open](https://github.com/GlassOnTin/civic-kernel/issues). Nothing here is
commercial, and nothing is asked beyond that.
