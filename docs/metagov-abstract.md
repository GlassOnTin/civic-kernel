## Title

**Nobody had to trust the shed: a thin protocol for public decisions,
stress-tested against fifty-two real institutional failures**

## Abstract

What is the *smallest* piece of democratic infrastructure that is still worth
having — and how would you know? The Civic Kernel is one worked answer: four
services, four verbs, two message formats, twelve refusals, and a floor of rights,
designed so that the protocol is too thin for anyone to profitably capture. The
refusals do the load-bearing work: the kernel never decides substance, never edits
the roll, never compels a remedy — because a machine that could would be the most
valuable thing in public life to own.

The evidence is an unusual corpus. Fifty-two documented institutional failures —
Post Office Horizon, government by disappearing WhatsApp, an exam graded by
postcode, a carer prosecuted for a debt the state watched grow — were rebuilt as
machine-validated case files and run against the protocol, each ending in an honest
verdict: 43 *strain* (it helps, with a hard limit named), 8 *break*, 1 *holds*. Two
findings will carry the talk. First, compressibility: fifty-two failures reduce to
six recurring shapes, and the protocol's core needed to grow exactly twice to carry
all of them — evidence that "thin" is an empirical property, not an aesthetic.
Second, honesty as architecture: the corpus counts its own failures, and the breaks
map the protocol's real preconditions — a record must be made, a forum must act,
an adopter must consent.

The demonstration is live: the audience verifies a sixty-member society's election
in their own browsers, from the published record alone, with a verifier that shares
no code with the election software — and then watches two riggings fail, the second
one (history erased and re-signed end to end by a fully colluding committee) caught
only by a fingerprint anchored outside everyone's reach.

For discussion, honestly posed: when does a governance protocol earn the right to
be thin? Is the compressibility finding real, or an artefact of a UK-shaped sample?
And where should such a thing live — the project's own architecture essay names
Metagov as the natural host for the interoperability conversation, so this
submission is the essay following its own recommendation. A standing adversarial
invitation is part of the offer: the tamper suite is public, and a winning attack
becomes a named defence.
