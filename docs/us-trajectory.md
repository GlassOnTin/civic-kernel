# The US trajectory

What this page is for: the corpus gives Germany and the United States the same two enum
values — remedy `strike`, scope `unconditional` — and the measured runs return the same
verdict, `strains`. This page walks the US ladder and finds that the identical enum is
held by a different tenure, and that the difference is the whole story. Germany's strike
is entrenched text applied by a court whose own structure is now entrenched; the US
strike is judge-made doctrine — Marbury, applied through Lukumi — held by a generalist
bench whose composition is a standing political prize. And where the UK declines its one
flipping rung and the EU has none left, the US's two remaining rungs are **locked in its
founding text**: pre-enforcement review at Article III, coverage at Article V. The
manifest says Germany and America sit on the same rung. What the manifest cannot say is
that one is bolted there and the other is standing on it.

The attack is fixed throughout: a 61% majority banning a minority's worship (the
[`majority-vs-minority`](../scenarios.html) series). Only the US configuration changes.

**The measured strain is the interim, and the interim is doctrine.** The US run's
distinctive cost against Germany's: the Act was presumed constitutional and *operated* —
Fatima Nasser's permit was revoked and the Delmar Avenue site closed — and only that
injury bought her a courtroom, because US review is post-hoc and adversarial, with no
abstract route. Germany's run needed no injured plaintiff: a quarter of the Bundestag
plus a Land government filed abstract review, and the statute entered the log
enacted-pending-review rather than biting anyone first. That German feature is exactly
the US's S3 — a pre-enactment gate, the guard's flag with somewhere to go
([§3.3](https://glassontin.github.io/civic-kernel/#s3-3 "A vote to strip someone's rights does not sail through at 51%. It is pushed onto a slower, louder, harder road — never silently blocked, never cheaply passed."))
— and it is not a statute away. Case-or-controversy is Article III jurisprudence: the
polity would have to amend the founding text to let anyone sue before someone is hurt.
The rung the German column gets from its constitution, the US constitution forbids.
Fourteen months, funded lawyers, and a revoked permit is what the same enum value costs
when the gate is welded shut — the run measured all three.

**Tenure: the same enum, a different half-life.** Nothing in the waist distinguishes
Germany's strike from America's, and everything in the two runs does. Germany's rests on
Art 79(3) — the ban cannot return with a bigger majority, and since December 2024 the
court's own structure is entrenched too. The US has no eternity clause: the measured run
records that a future supermajority could re-enact by amendment what the Court struck.
But the nearer exposure is cheaper than Article V and the run names it: the strike that
saved Riverton is an application of *Lukumi*, and precedent is held by whoever holds the
bench. The same majority that lost 9–0 today can, over years, reshape who reads
'neutral and generally applicable' — no text changes, no amendment, and the enum still
says `strike` the day it stops meaning one. This is the compression the remedy field
cannot express, and the German page arrives at it from the opposite side: **entrenchment
— what holds the remedy in place — is a candidate axis of its own**, motivated jointly by
the pair the way the EU run motivated `scope`. Noted for the model; deliberately not
added to the schema here.

**Coverage, narrow and deep — orthogonal here, decisive elsewhere.** 14/30 is the
thinnest coverage of any measured column: no socio-economic rights at all, no federal
right to education, nothing for UDHR 22–28. It did not matter to this attack — 2, 7 and
18 are backed, and remedy did the work — but the trajectory should say plainly what the
column means when the attack changes domain: an American majority stripping a minority's
*housing* or *healthcare* trips no invariant the deployment declares, and the guard has
nothing to flag. The manifest renders that honestly
([§6](https://glassontin.github.io/civic-kernel/#s6 "Nobody has to adopt all of it. Any group may take the parts it wants — but must publish, in a form anyone's phone can read, exactly what it left out.")):
narrow-and-deep is a legible position on the lattice, and Article V is why it is also,
in practice, a fixed one.

**S2 is not hypothetical here.** For every other column the verifiable-ballot rung is an
upgrade; for the US it is the live wound — the seating of the chamber is the subject of
its running political crisis, its recount wars, its election-administration litigation.
The UK pair measured the rung orthogonal to a T7 verdict *when the majority genuinely
holds the votes*, and load-bearing against a faction that merely claims to — and the US
is the measured polity where that second case is the standing national anxiety. Cheap,
reversible, sovereignty-free, and nowhere more worth taking.

That completes the set, five polities, five different jams: the UK at the **remedy** (a
sovereign that will not bind itself), the EU at **scope and obedience** (unanimity, and a
rung that is not on the ladder), the PRC at the **record itself**, Germany at **nothing**
— the ceiling held, the bench the residue — and the US at its **founding text**, Articles
III and V, with its strike held by precedent in the meantime. Same ladder, five jams, and
the jam tells you what kind of polity you are looking at.
